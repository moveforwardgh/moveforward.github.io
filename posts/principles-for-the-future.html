Principles for the future

December 11, 2020

Every transformation process is guided by main principles, which serve as assumptions of what social reality is and how it should be, which can lead to different frameworks and socio-organizational models.

We explore some of the principles here that should be considered pillars to create a future based on equity, sustainability, technology and co-manage of organizations, which we need to orient solutions to the macro problems that we face.

General principless

Scientific thinking. Reliance in the methods of science as the best way to know and understand reality, wich also imples the rejection of tradition, mysticism and other types of thinking as a way to comprehend the world.

Tecnhnology reliance. Confidence in technical application and technoproducts, especially engineering to effectively solve problems.

Sustainability. Focus on finding a balance that guarantees the sustainability of systems and processes.

Environmentalism. Focus on active protection and restoration of the environment.

Equality. Perspective in which the equity of resources, opportunities and living standards are prioritized according to the criteria of possibilities and capabilities.

Horizontalism Focus on creating, developing and maintaining horizontal social structures that can enable a dynamic self-governance platform.

Globalism.Emphasis on creating and connecting structures on a global scale that can enable a common planetary civilization.

Co-decisionism. Decisions on strategic social issues must be managed jointly.

Post-laborism. Rejection of the idea of ​​the working class - or the proletariat - as the main subject of historical evolution.

Post-humanism. A post-anthropocentric, system-centric and transhumanist perspective of agents and societies.

Practical principles

Participacionism. oint decision and self-governance regarding the specific activities that take place in society, both in their understanding and design, as well as in the implementation and evaluation of decisions.

Scientific-oriented decisions. Decision guided by multidisciplinary science and systemic knowledge, such as evidence-based policies, expert commissions or similar methods.

Informational support. Guidance through information technology to allow more efficient decision-making.

Sectoralism. As it is impossible for everyone to decide on everything, sectorized decision-making mechanisms are to be implemented in areas that are not strategic or of collective relevance.

Global-localism. Strategic decisions that have a collective impact should be made on a global scale, and on the other hand, issues with local implications and self-determination should be made on a local scale.

Automatization. Focus on the mechanization and automation of production and distribution through the use of advanced technology, such as robotics, 3D printing, artificial intelligence and other emerging possibilities.

Sustainable implementation. Prioritization and emphasis of green energies, absence of pollution, cyclical-circular models of production and preservation of ecosystems.

Sustainable living. Search for a balanced life internally and with the ecosystem.

Science-art synthesis. Break with the dichotomy of art and science, in which scientific knowledge and its application can be mixed with forms of art and expression.

Animalism. Defense of the rights of non-human animals and their respective ecosystems and habitats where they coexist.

Same principles, different possibilities

The principles can give rise to different organizational models, which, due to a matter of complexity, can never be pre-designed, however they do mark general directions that give rise to different forms, models and socio-organizational systems with different natures.
